Ciao, I'm Jack, a community college transfer in their final year of undergraduate studies. Born with deafness in my right ear, my eye is always focusing on the visual field: whether its graphics, film, photography, natural/cultural landscapes, color, etc. etc. etc. I turned my hearing loss into a powerful vessel by combining my intense observation of landscapes and visual eye into geographic practice and cartographic representation :) I spend most of my days trying to tell people what the "cartographic" is, and why it matters. Otherwise, I surf, bike, root for the Giants and Kings, read books of all kinds, and obssess over cities and their form. And Teju Cole, Doreen Massey always.

Reading Review

  • Developing an eye for design is a matter of learning to see. Most design- regardles of profession/discipline- produces affective responses cognitively. It's most evident when we watch a beautiful film and feel apart of its atmosphere, but this reaction sparks a "How did they do that?" question. All design disciplines have their own constraints to overcome. For me, it is Cartographic Design that I am always developing an eye for as I study more in-depth and engage with more well-designed maps. I appreciate Warner's approach of 'developing an eye' since I am constantly doing this with maps: placing myself in another Cartographer's position, deciphering how they developed a design solution.
  • Warner's section on "Don't just use apps, study them" is informative on how to develop a way of seeing in design. Language builds the image, and here Warner offers insightful questions to ask while studying design. These questions establish a foundation upon which to build from throughout the semester as I learn to design websites that are both functional and aesthetic to the user. This language then allows me to dig deeper into design principles being executed in the interface.
  • "I was obsessed with copying pieces of the trending Dribbble shots, but oblivious to how all the pieces worked together." This quote is simple yet has so much depth. It speaks to a trend in multiple industries where there is a growing lack of genuine inellectual approaches to practice, with the sage advice being 'just hack it' and 'google it'. While copying/forking/googling has its merits at times, its difficult to proclaim 'I am ____' without an appreciation of a discipline's function, aesthetics, and politics.
  • 8. The article was concise and informative about engaging UI/UX design in everyday elements. Additionally, it is always pleasing to see professionals acknowledge their initial shortcomings in their career, and how they shifted their mindset to practice. However, I am a total nerd and do appreciate more complex articles that totally reorient my way of thinking. In the future, I would appreciate more in-depth readings about design as I am choosing to take the DeCal for desingn purposes. There should be a mix of both: quick, informative pieces from industry, and longer academic pieces (textbook, theory, etc.) which should sit on our minds as we design and code.
